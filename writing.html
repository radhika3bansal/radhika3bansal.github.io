Justice at What Cost? Penalties, Rape Deterrence,
and the Incentive to Murder

Increasing the penalties for a serious non-fatal crime can cost lives if the surviving victims are used as witnesses to convict the perpetrator of the non-fatal crime. In this paper, we consider the case of rape laws. First, we present a theoretical model to examine how increasing the penalty for one crime may lead to the perpetrator to commit a collateral crime when the latter crime reduces the probability of conviction of the former. We then consider the cases of spousal rape laws and the mandatory minimum 25 years sentence for molestation against a child. Both treatments were introduced in the USA in a staggered fashion, and increased the penalties for rape. We show, by using difference-in-differences design, that both treatments triggered an increase, respectively, in spousal homicides and child homicides.

with Shabana Mitra

Patriarchal Norms and Women's Work

Patriarchal norms continue to shape women's opportunities and obligations in many low- and middle-income countries, often limiting their role in household and community decision-making. This paper examines whether large-scale public employment can unsettle such norms by expanding women's access to paid work. Drawing on nationally representative data from the India Human Development Survey and the phased implementation of the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, we examine whether greater labour force participation erodes both sticky and non-sticky forms of patriarchy. Using a local randomization regression discontinuity design, we provide evidence that large-scale public employment programs can catalyse shifts in social expectations around women's economic roles, contributing to a gradual reconfiguration of household and community gender relations.
